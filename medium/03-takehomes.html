Data Engineering Take-Homes and Screeners: What Kohl's, Andela, and a Consulting Firm Actually Graded

Field notes from three real data engineering screens (2026): a live Python assessment at Kohl's, a client-screening take-home via Andela, and a consulting firm's async video screener. The pattern across all three: boring correctness wins. Stdlib, readable, handles the messy rows. Nobody awards points for cleverness.

Kohl's — live coding, Python, stdlib only

Plain Python — pandas and numpy explicitly banned, collections and datetime encouraged.

The question: a flat list holds purchase records — every 4 elements are one transaction: SKU, item description, price ('$10.00'), purchase date ('YYYY-MM-DD'). Print one formatted string: "{most_common_sku} {average_price} {min_date} {max_date}" — the average price of the most common SKU to 2 decimals, plus that SKU's first and last purchase dates.

purchases = [
    '123456', 'Nike T-shirt', '$12.65', '2026-01-01',
    '194850', 'Vans Shoes',   '$89.99', '2026-01-04',
    # … 10 rows total
]

def aggregate_purchase_data(data):
    sku_info = {}
    for i in range(0, len(data), 4):
        sku   = data[i]
        price = float(data[i + 2].replace('$', ''))
        date  = data[i + 3]
        info = sku_info.setdefault(sku,
            {"count": 0, "total": 0.0, "min_date": date, "max_date": date})
        info["count"] += 1
        info["total"] += price
        info["min_date"] = min(info["min_date"], date)
        info["max_date"] = max(info["max_date"], date)

    top  = max(sku_info, key=lambda s: sku_info[s]["count"])
    info = sku_info[top]
    return f"{top} {info['total']/info['count']:.2f} {info['min_date']} {info['max_date']}"

What they're testing: stride iteration over a flat list (range(0, len(data), 4)), string-to-number cleaning, and one-pass aggregation into a dict — GROUP BY in plain Python. The nice catch: ISO dates compare correctly as strings, so no datetime parsing is needed — saying that out loud scores points. Useful drill: solve it in SQL first, then translate.

Andela — client-screening take-home

Two parts, provided datasets. Graded on judgment, not code volume: how you handle nulls, dupes, and join mismatches — and whether you wrote the decisions down.

Part 1 — messy user records. A spreadsheet of synthetic user records (name, email, two phone columns, address, date of birth), deliberately dirty: missing values in every column, inconsistent phone formats, near-duplicate people. They want a repeatable cleaning pipeline — standardize formats, define a dedupe key, decide explicitly what happens to unfixable rows, document every decision. No hand-edits.

Part 2 — usage analytics. Two CSVs to join on username:

usage_data.csv: date, username, feature, sessions, time_spent, average_time_spent
user_data.csv:  first_name, last_name, username, role, region, subscription_plan

Landmines: subscription_plan has blanks, and the join key only exists cleanly on one side if Part 1 was done right. Typical asks: usage by feature/role/region, free-trial vs paid behavior, most and least engaged segments — presented as findings, not just tables. Keep it reproducible: one runnable script or notebook plus a short README of assumptions.

The async video screener — 4 questions, one take

A consulting firm's pre-interview step: one self-recorded video, 2–3 minutes total, covering four questions, emailed in. They explicitly said no need to make it polished or formal — they're looking for authenticity.

  1. What specifically interests you about this opportunity?
  2. What accomplishment are you most proud of and why?
  3. Share a brief example of a project where requirements were unclear. How did you create structure, drive progress, and keep stakeholders aligned?
  4. What's a recent industry trend that excites you and why?

What works, learned by doing it:

  • Q2 wants one specific, quantifiable story — a real system, real numbers, three concrete technical decisions. Not a career montage.
  • Q3 is a process question in disguise. The strong shape: the mistake you used to make → the reframe (walk the stakeholder through a real scenario end-to-end, then mirror back your understanding as a diagram) → proof you now reuse that pattern.
  • Q4 is a depth check. Pick a trend you've actually touched and land one non-obvious insight. "AI is exciting" is a zero.
  • Mechanics matter: light in front of you, plain background, look at the lens for each answer's open and close. One take with small stumbles lands better than a memorized script.
